ABSTRACT
Electron densities calculated for a variety of systems by means of density functional theory (DFT) are compared with the results given by several conventional ab initio methods, including Hartree-Fock (HF), second-order Møller-Plesset perturbation theory (MP2) and quadratic configuration interaction including single and double excitations (QCISD). The latter is used as a reference by which the accuracy of the DFT electron densities is measured. Among the properties discussed, particular attention is paid to certain topological properties, the Laplacian of the electron density and DFT spin densities. The chapter concludes with a suggestion for an approach for the development of new DFT functionals.

1. Introduction
Density functional theory (DFT) $^{1,2,3}$ is an attractive alternative method for the study of the electronic structure of molecules. Even the simplest approximation of DFT, the local density approximation (LDA), yields molecular structures comparable to those obtained from conventional ab initio correlated methods. The gradient-corrected density functional methods yield energies for molecular processes which agree to within a few kcal/mol with high-level ab initio results. Computational efficiency and small disk requirements make DFT a useful tool for studying molecular systems which are too large to be treated by conventional post-Hartree-Fock methods.

According to the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem,4 the ground state energy and other molecular properties are uniquely defined by the electron density. Since the electron density is a physically observable quantity, any theoretical method, DFT or ab initio, should yield the same electron density in the limit of an exact treatment. David J. Tozer and Nicholas C. Handy

Department of Chemistry

Abstract
Exchange-Correlation potentials, computed using the method of Zhao, Morrison, and Parr, have been used to train a computational neural network. The resulting local potential has been implemented in a self-consistent Kohn-Sham procedure, and optimised geometries, total energies and Kohn-Sham eigenvalues computed for a variety of molecular systems. Bond angles and A-H bond lengths are more accurate with the neural network than with regular LSDA and BLYP. This improvement is, however, associated with a less accurate description of A-B bond lengths. The neural network highest occupied eigenvalues are significantly better than for LSDA and BLYP, although total energies from the network are more negative than with these conventional functionals. This observation can be traced to the use of a local approximation for a potential which mimics the exact value.

The computational simplicity of Kohn-Sham density functional theory (DFT) $^{1,2}$ arises from the use of the one-electron density $\rho(\mathbf{r})$ as the basic quantity. Given the density, all molecular properties can be obtained, including the exchange-correlation potential. The calculation of this potential is a very active research area $^{3-11}$ . Ingamells and Handy $^{10}$ recently outlined preliminary calculations

2.2 Computational Neural Networks
The ZMP method provides a large amount of data in the form $\rho (\mathbf{r}_i),\nabla \rho (\mathbf{r}i)$ $\ldots ,v{xc}(\mathbf{r}_i)$ , where $\mathbf{r}_i$ refers to spatial grid points in various molecular systems.

The aim of using a neural network is to fit the exchange-correlation potential at the points to the density information at those points. A neural network consists of a set of input nodes, a set of output nodes, and sets of hidden nodes. The nodes in one layer are connected to all the nodes in the subsequent and previous layers, and each connection is associated with a weight. It is through these weights that the fitting flexibility is achieved, with nodes performing non-linear operations (typically through the hyperbolic tangent function) on the weighted sums of the outputs from the previous nodes.

If we wish to perform geometry optimisations then it is desirable that the neural network exchange-correlation potential is a functional derivative of a translationally invariant energy functional (otherwise there would be a net force on the molecule complicating the optimisation $^{18}$ ). For this reason we use a neural network which maps just the density at the points to the exchange-correlation potentials at those points, since a local potential will always be a functional derivative. Our neural network therefore has one input node $(\rho(\mathbf{r}i))$ , one output node $(v{xc}(\mathbf{r}_i))$ , and a single hidden layer of 8 nodes. Before the neural network can be used for calculations on arbitrary systems it must be trained. This is achieved by providing values of $\rho_0(\mathbf{r}i)$ and $v{xc}(\mathbf{r}_i)$ at a large number of points for a variety of molecules, and employing a 'back propagation' technique to vary the network weights such that the difference between the supplied and predicted exchange-correlation potentials is minimised. In our calculations we use the BackProB neural network code of Oldfield and MacKay $^{19}$ .

Once trained, the neural network can be implemented into a regular Kohn-Sham procedure. The iterating density is passed to the neural network, and the exchange-correlation potential is returned. The Kohn-Sham matrix is then constructed in the usual manner and the algorithm continues to self-consistency.
