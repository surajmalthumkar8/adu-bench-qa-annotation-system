1. Brief Historical Introduction
During the last hundred years, the discussion about the concepts of space and time and their interrelation has been very intense in physics, and the literature is enormous. After the establishment of Maxwell's equations in 1873 and the introduction of the concept of the ether, the question of the motion of the earth with respect to the ether became of fundamental importance. The negative outcome of the Michelson-Morley [1] experiment was a great surprise, which indicated that the velocity of light may be the same in all moving systems independent of the velocity of the light source. In order to explain this peculiar phenomenon, several physicists introduced the idea of "contractions" of moving bodies later leading to linear couplings between space and time: FitzGerald [2], Lorentz [3], Larmor [4], and Poincaré [5], and these relations are now known as the Lorentz transformations. In studying systems moving with respect to each other, the old Galilei transformations were then replaced by the Lorentz-transformations for all systems of inertia. Poincaré seems also to have been one of the first to postulate the principle of relativity that the laws of physics should be the same in all these moving systems. When Einstein [6] established his special theory of relativity in 1905, one of the main purposes was to show that the laws of electrodynamics as expressed by Maxwell's equations were invariant under these transformations as a consequence of the assumption that the velocity of light is the same in all systems independent of the velocity of the light source. The theory was given an elegant and forceful geometrical interpretation by Minkowski [7] in 1909. Since the new concepts meant a revolution not only in electrodynamics but in the entire physics, it was important to investigate the fundamental assumptions for the new theory in great detail. One of the first straightforward derivations of the Lorentz-transformations was given by Pauli [8] in 1921 in his famous encyclopedia paper, and it was based on the principle of relativity and the following four assumptions:

(I). The velocity of light is the same in all privileged systems.

(II). The properties of space and time:

a. space and time are homogeneous,

b. space is isotropic,

c. space is symmetric with respect to velocities.

Again the starting point is the negative outcome of the Michelson-Morley experiment, and the derivation is hence closely associated with the phenomenon of light. There are many other derivations [9], and practically all of them are based on similar assumptions. For more references, the reader is referred to the standard textbooks on the Special Theory of Relativity.

It is remarkable that Einstein in the same volume of Annalen der Physik in 1905 published not only his paper about the special theory of relativity, but also his papers about the photoelectric effect and the Brownian motion, which was a tremendous achievement. However, many scientist working in other parts of physics were in the following decades greatly disturbed by the fact that their basic concepts were modified to meet the requirements of electrodynamics, and it is perhaps significant that, when Einstein obtained the Nobel prize in physics in the beginning of the 1920's—it was the 1921 reserved prize awarded in 1922—he got it "for his services to theoretical physics, and particularly for his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect" and not for his theory of relativity. In the study of the photoelectric effect, Einstein had assumed that the electromagnetic radiation was quantized and occurred in wave packets with the energy $E = h\nu$ .

Quantum theory had been introduced by Planck in 1900, and the first Nobel prize in this area was awarded to him in 1918, the second prize was granted to Einstein in 1921, whereas the third prize was given to Bohr in 1922, which seems to establish some form of logical order for these recognitions.
