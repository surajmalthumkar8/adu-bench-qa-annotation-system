The evidence establishing first order melting of the vortex lattice in clean $\mathrm{YBa}_2\mathrm{Cu}3\mathrm{O}{7 - \delta}$ is reviewed. Connections between the thermodynamic phase transition and non-thermodynamic transport properties are demonstrated.

1 Introduction
The vortex phase diagram of high temperature superconductors is rich in equilibrium and dynamic phases and phase transitions. [1,2] Depending on extrinsic parameters, the mixed state can be theoretically described by any of a multitude of phases including liquid, hexatic liquid, ordered lattice, disordered solid including vortex, Bose and polymer glasses, and liquid crystal-like nematic and smectic states. The equilibrium phases of the vortex system are postulated to be separated by first and second order phase transitions, while the steady states of driven vortex matter are expected to be separated by novel dynamic phase transitions. One of the proposed phase transitions is now well-established through a variety of experimental techniques. In clean samples the transition of a vortex liquid into a vortex solid has been experimentally shown to be a first order thermodynamic phase transition. [4,5] The experimental tools which have been developed to establish first order melting can be applied and extended to investigate many additional features of the equilibrium and dynamic phase diagrams.

Fig.1(a) shows the generic phase diagram theoretically expected for vortex systems without pinning, while Fig.1(b) shows the experimental phase diagram now established for clean $\mathrm{YBa}_2\mathrm{Cu}3\mathrm{O}{7 - \delta}$ by magnetization, calorimetric, and transport measurements. The calorimetric and magnetization experiments probe the vortices at rest. As long as the experiment is reversible, these measurements provide thermodynamic information on latent heat or heat capacity and changes in vortex density, respectively. In contrast, the resistivity is sensitive to vortices in motion, a non-equilibrium state, providing information on the vortex velocity.
